The history of the American flag is a reflection of the nation's growth and transformation, marked by the addition of stars to represent new states joining the Union. The journey begins in 1777 with the Continental Colors, which featured 13 stars, symbolizing the original 13 colonies: Delaware, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Georgia, Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maryland, South Carolina, New Hampshire, Virginia, New York, North Carolina, and Rhode Island. Early variations of the flag, such as the Francis Hopkinson, Betsy Ross, and Cowpens flags, each displayed these 13 stars. As the nation expanded, the flag evolved, with the addition of stars and stripes to represent new states. By 1795, the flag had 15 stars and 15 stripes, following the inclusion of Vermont and Kentucky, and the iconic 15-star variant inspired Francis Scott Key's “Defence of Fort M'Henry.”

In 1818, the flag was updated to 20 stars to accommodate the new states of Tennessee, Ohio, Louisiana, Indiana, and Mississippi. This was followed by the Great Star flag, a 20-star variant, and later the 21-star flag with Illinois in 1820. The flag continued to grow, with new stars added for Alabama, Maine, Missouri, Arkansas, Michigan, Florida, Texas, Iowa, Wisconsin, and more, reflecting the expansion of the United States westward. Notably, during the 1840s, the Great Star flag, a 26-star variant, symbolized a growing nation.

In the mid-1800s, the flag saw the addition of stars for California, Minnesota, Oregon, and Kansas, followed by the 35-star flag in 1863, which represented West Virginia’s entry. With each new state, the design of the flag evolved, with stars being added, as seen with the 36-star flag following Nevada's statehood and the 37-star flag after Nebraska joined in 1867. By 1877, the flag reached 38 stars with Colorado's admission, and the addition of new states continued into the late 19th century, culminating in the 43-star flag in 1891, reflecting North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, Washington, and Idaho.

By the 20th century, the flag saw further updates with the 44-star flag for Wyoming in 1896, the 45-star flag for Utah in 1908, and the 46-star flag for Oklahoma in 1912. The flag continued to change in the mid-1900s, with New Mexico and Arizona bringing the total to 48 stars by 1959. Finally, Alaska's statehood in 1959 added the 49th star, and in 1960, Hawaii became the 50th state, completing the flag we recognize today. Through each addition of stars, the American flag has become a symbol of unity, growth, and the ever-expanding nature of the United States.
